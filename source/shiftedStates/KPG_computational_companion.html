Problem setup

A natural question to ask about quantum graphs is "What happens to my favorite PDE when I place it on a quantum graph?" In a series of papers, Kairzhan and Pelinovsky considered the propagation of waves satisfying the cubic NLS equation on star-shaped quantum graphs. A fundamental property of the cubic NLS

is that it possesses an infinite set of conservation laws. Consider the star graph consisting of half-lines emanating from a single vertex, with Kirchhoff boundary conditions. When NLS is defined on the star graph subject to Kirchhoff boundary condition

About the numerics

The theorems proved in this paper apply to star graphs consisting of three or more half lines that meet at a single vertex. We truncated each edge to a finite length and then modified the Laplacian operator on a small segment near the end, using the method of perfectly matched layers following a paper of Nissen and Kreiss.2 I remember that at the time I was working at this, I found that radiation shed by the solution was reflected off the endpoints of the truncated half lines and changing the results. For the time stepping, we used a split-step method due to Weidemann and Herbst.3 The two terms on the right-hand side of Eq. () are handled separately. At each time step of length one first evolves the linear part of the equation by a time step of length using the Crank-Nicholson method. Note that in our implementation of the finite difference scheme, the central vertex lies halfway between spatial grid points, and there is no need to explicitly satisfy the boundary condition. Then one evolves the nonlinearity by a time step of length . Putting into polar coordinates, this is simply a pointwise update of the phase. One then evolves the linear part by another time step of length . Using the standard trick, we may combine all the intermediate half-time-steps, and perform half as many linear solves over the course of the solution.
